## Module 1 
**Focus:**
This module establishes the core triad of cybersecurity—confidentiality, integrity, and availability—while emphasizing that effective security is fundamentally about risk management, not just technology. Key takeaways include the importance of multi-factor authentication, password hygiene, and understanding that human behavior remains the most frequently exploited vulnerability.

**What I did:**
I analyzed real-world data breach scenarios to understand how failures in confidentiality, integrity, and availability lead to security incidents. I mapped my own personal security habits—specifically password reuse and inconsistent use of multi-factor authentication—to potential attack vectors that threat actors commonly exploit. I also evaluated case studies including the Stuxnet attack to understand how cyberwarfare extends beyond data theft to cause physical damage to critical infrastructure. Finally, I documented risk assessment principles and applied them across personal, organizational, and government security levels.

**Outcome:**
I developed a security-first mindset grounded in risk management rather than reactive fear-based responses. I established a personal security baseline that includes unique passwords for every account, multi-factor authentication enabled on all critical services, and a consistently skeptical approach to unsolicited links and email attachments. Most importantly, I learned to articulate cybersecurity as a shared responsibility where both individual users and organizations must actively participate in defense.

**Core Competencies Demonstrated:**
Risk assessment methodology, multi-factor authentication implementation, security mindset development, critical infrastructure protection awareness

## Module 2 
**Focus:**
This module explores the technical landscape of cyber threats, including malware variants (ransomware, worms, Trojans), attack techniques (social engineering, DoS/DDoS, on-path attacks), and system vulnerabilities (buffer overflows, Meltdown, Spectre). The module reinforces that attackers exploit both software flaws and human trust, making layered defenses essential.

**What I did:**
I classified major malware families—including ransomware, worms, Trojans, rootkits, and spyware—by their behavior, propagation methods, and typical payloads. I simulated social engineering scenarios to identify common tactics such as pretexting, quid pro quo, and tailgating in real-world contexts. I analyzed system vulnerabilities ranging from buffer overflows and race conditions to hardware-level exploits including Meltdown, Spectre, and Rowhammer. I also investigated emerging threats such as cryptojacking and botnet-driven distributed denial-of-service attacks to understand how attackers adapt to new technologies.

**Outcome:**
I built a functional threat taxonomy that enables rapid identification of attack patterns during security events or investigations. I learned that technical controls alone are never sufficient, as user awareness and behavioral caution remain critical defense layers against social engineering and phishing. I can now differentiate between white hat, gray hat, and black hat attacker motivations and methods, which helps in both threat hunting and security posture assessment.

**Core Competencies Demonstrated:**
Malware classification and behavior analysis, social engineering detection, hardware and software vulnerability assessment, emerging threat monitoring

## Module 3
**Focus:**
This module focuses on practical data protection strategies, including device and network hardening, encryption, backup protocols, and privacy management. Key competencies include securing wireless networks, using VPNs on public Wi-Fi, implementing two-factor authentication, and understanding how terms of service affect data ownership.

**What I did:**
I configured wireless network security controls in a lab environment, including WPA2 encryption, SSID hardening, and router password changes to reduce unauthorized access risks. I implemented backup strategies combining local external drives with cloud storage, ensuring both redundancy and encryption for sensitive files. I reviewed Terms of Service and privacy policies from major online platforms to identify how user data is collected, stored, shared, and sometimes resold. I also enabled two-factor authentication and OAuth on personal accounts, then practiced secure browsing habits including VPN usage, private browsing modes, and disabling Bluetooth when not in use.

**Outcome:**
I established repeatable data protection workflows that include regular scheduled backups, encryption for all sensitive files, and proper permanent deletion protocols rather than simple file deletion. I learned that default privacy settings almost always favor platform convenience over user protection, making active configuration mandatory for anyone serious about data privacy. I can now advise non-technical users on practical privacy measures without overwhelming them with technical jargon, translating complex concepts into actionable steps.

**Core Competencies Demonstrated:**
Wireless network hardening, backup and encryption protocols, privacy policy analysis, two-factor authentication deployment, secure browsing practices

## Module 4 
**Focus:**
This module examines organizational security through the lens of security appliances (firewalls, IDS/IPS), continuous monitoring (NetFlow, penetration testing), and incident response frameworks (CSIRT, SIEM, DLP). The module highlights that mature security programs balance prevention with detection, response, and continuous improvement.

**What I did:**
I practiced the full incident response lifecycle—detect, analyze, contain, eradicate, recover—using simulated security alert scenarios that mimicked real-world breaches. I investigated alerts using SIEM and IDS tools in a controlled learning environment, learning to distinguish between false positives and genuine threats. I documented my findings and maintained structured incident records following industry reporting standards used by professional CSIRT teams. I studied different firewall types including NAT, proxy, and host-based firewalls, as well as intrusion prevention systems for network defense. I also reviewed the complete penetration testing methodology: planning and reconnaissance, scanning and enumeration, gaining access, maintaining persistence, and final analysis with reporting.

**Outcome:**
I built foundational, hands-on skills in security monitoring, alert triage, and incident response workflows that translate directly to SOC analyst responsibilities. I understand how security appliances—firewalls, IDS/IPS, data loss prevention systems, and SIEM platforms—work together as a layered defense architecture rather than as isolated tools. I am prepared to participate in CSIRT-style response activities and contribute meaningfully to security playbook execution, including post-incident forensic analysis and backdoor removal.

**Core Competencies Demonstrated:**
Incident response lifecycle execution, SIEM and IDS alert investigation, firewall and IPS configuration, penetration testing methodology, forensic documentation

## Module 5
**Focus:**
This module addresses the legal and ethical obligations of cybersecurity professionals, including compliance, corporate codes of conduct, and ethical decision-making frameworks. Career development pathways are mapped through recognized certifications (CompTIA Security+, CEH, CISSP, Cisco CyberOps) and resources such as CyberSeek for market intelligence.

**What I did:**
I applied ethical decision-making frameworks to real-world case scenarios, testing each potential action against legal compliance, corporate policy, stakeholder impact, universal standard suitability, and public newsworthiness. I reviewed corporate codes of conduct from Cisco alongside professional ethics guidelines from the Information Systems Security Association to understand industry expectations. I mapped certification pathways including entry-level credentials (CCST, CompTIA Security+), ethical hacking certifications (CEH), advanced credentials (CISSP), and operations-focused certifications (Cisco CyberOps Associate). I also used CyberSeek and major job boards to analyze role requirements, skill gaps, and salary benchmarks for positions such as SOC Analyst, IT Security Specialist, and Junior Penetration Tester.

**Outcome:** 
I recognize that legal compliance and ethical judgment are non-negotiable professional obligations in cybersecurity—technical skill alone is never sufficient for responsible practice. I developed a personal career roadmap that begins with CompTIA Security+ to meet DoD requirements, progresses through hands-on certifications like CyberOps or CEH, and targets long-term advancement toward CISSP. I can articulate the importance of transparency, root cause analysis, and continuous improvement after security incidents, understanding that how an organization responds to a breach is often as important as how it prevents one.

**Core Competencies Demonstrated:**
Ethical decision-making frameworks, legal compliance awareness, corporate code of conduct application, certification roadmap planning, job market analysis
